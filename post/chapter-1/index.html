Project #1 Machine Learning approaches in investigating trait divergence under species diversification at different taxonomic scales in wild Helianthus species

January 5, 2021

Trait based identification in plants using dichotomous keys have seen widespread use in biology (Tilling 1984, Edwards et al. 1987) because they are a convenient and an inexpensive method of species identification when tissue sample collection is not be feasible (Will and Rubinoff 2004). Improving this process of identification by way of reducing the number of traits required can be incredibly beneficial and cost effective for big projects where the use of genetic tools might not be possible (Gallo and Waitt, 2011; Roy et al., 2016). Almeida et al. (2020) demonstrated the feasibility of using machine learning algorithms in the trait-based species classification of species and the identification of the most relevant traits. Machine learning algorithms can be used to build predictive models (Valletta 2016 et al.) and are characterized by its ability to learn from patterns in the data and make future predictions based on the observed patterns (Nivedha and Sairam 2015). Unlike traditional statistical models, machine learning predictive models can handle non-linear relationships in the data (Bland et al. 2014) and are useful in the context of high dimensional complex datasets (Valletta et al. 2016). Supervised, semi-supervised and unsupervised are the three main types of machine learning algorithms (Libbrecht and Noble 2015). Supervised machine learning algorithms are trained on labelled data (Libbrecht and Noble 2015) and the goal is to improve the predictive accuracy on previously unseen data (Valletta 2016 et al.). The labelled data can be from two distinct classes or from multiple classes, hence it is possible to build a predictive machine learning model for binary and multi-class classification (Santhanam et al. 2016). Classification is the process by which data-points or objects are mapped or “classified” into the corresponding predefined class labels for a given dataset (Sarker et al. 2019).

In this present study we demonstrate the efficacy of a machine learning based approach in identifying and using potentially most divergent functional traits with regards to species classification for Helianthus at a genus and clade-level. Polyploidy and hybridization are commonplace in the Helianthus genus, however the species used in this study represent a vast majority of the diploid nonhybrid taxa (Timme et al., 2007). The species used in this study fall into three main clades, (a) Annual (b) Perennial and (c) South-eastern perennial (Stephens et al. 2015). Plant functional traits are shaped by the interaction with biotic and abiotic components of the environment, and these traits influence growth, survival, and reproduction (Caruso et al. 2020). Functional traits in land plants often differentiate across multivariate trait spectra which includes stress tolerance, plant size, reproductive timing (Westoby et al. 2002; Diaz et al. 2004; Reich et al. 2014). For example, Mason and Donovan (2015) demonstrated, using a phylogenetic comparative method approach, the differentiation of leaf physiological traits along the leaf economic spectrum in 28 species of the Helianthus genus.

Similar to Almeida et al. (2020), this study leverages the inherent interpretability of decision trees to build the predictive models (Hutchinson and Gigerenzer 2005), however ensemble methods like random forests (Breiman, 2001a; Cutler et al. 2007) and gradient boosting machines were also used to combat overfitting in decision trees (Valletta et al. 2016) to build better predictive models. Furthermore, this study only pertains to the genus Helianthus and the 28 nonhybrid species across three different clades within it. The main aim of this study can thus be summed up into three main over-arching questions:

  1. What are the potentially divergent traits at a Genus level?
  2. What are the potentially divergent traits at the clade level? Are these the same traits or does sunflowers diversify across different trait axes at a clade level?
  3. Can we use these potentially divergent traits to classify species accurately using a tree-based machine learning approach?

Project #4 Using Machine Learning and GIS to gain novel insight into crop sunflower cultivation

January 12, 2017

Problem Statement

This study centered around answering three pertinent questions about crop sunflower cultivation in the U.S. These were:

  1. What are the most important weather factors and soil characteristics that tends to influence seed yield?

  2. What are the most important weather and soil characteristics that tends to influence crop failure?

  3. Is it possible to build a predictive model that would be able to predict seed yield and crop failure values close to the actual historical values by taking the most important weather factors and soil characteristics as input?

Introduction

Crop sunflower is an important agricultural crop and is grown globally as an oilseed. In the United States, crop sunflower is one of the most important agricultural crops, after soybean and corn. General agricultural practices of a specific year, weather and soil characteristics of a specific geographical location are influential in the overall crop yield and crop failure. This study aimed to elucidate the important geographic, weather and soil factors that has potentially influenced crop yield and crop failure in the U.S. over previous decades by using a machine learning approach. Climate variables like maximum temperature, minimum temperature, and precipitation during the growing period as well as longitude and year of planting were shown to be the most influential in determining seed yield and they explained about 60% of the variation. Total planting area in addition to maximum and minimum temperatures along with precipitation during the growing period were deemed to be important factors and these variables explained about 35% of the variation. Two predictive models were then built, one for predicting yield and one for crop failure, using only the most influential variables. The root mean square error when predicting seed yield on the unseen test dataset was 0.69 and it was 0.80 when predicting crop failure. A GIS approach was used to spatially visualize the accuracy of these predictions with respect to specific U.S. counties.
